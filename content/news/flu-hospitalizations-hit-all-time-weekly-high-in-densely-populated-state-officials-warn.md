<p>The <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/us/us-regions/northeast/new-york" target="_blank" rel="noopener">New York State Department of Health</a> has announced the highest number of flu hospitalizations recorded in a single week.</p><p>The state confirmed the uptick in hospital visits in a press release on Jan. 2, as flu cases continue to rise in the region and nationwide.</p><p>New York State, including New York City, has consistently tracked the highest numbers of recorded <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/respiratory-health" target="_blank" rel="noopener">respiratory illness cases</a> in the country for the last few weeks, according to CDC data. Several other states have climbed to the "very high" category for respiratory activity as well, as of the week ending Dec. 27.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/health/record-breaking-flu-numbers-reported-new-york-state-sparking-warnings-from-officials" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>RECORD-BREAKING FLU NUMBERS REPORTED IN NEW YORK STATE, SPARKING WARNINGS FROM OFFICIALS</strong></a></p><p>The most recent data from the New York health department showed a total of 4,546 hospitalizations from Dec. 26, 2025, to Jan. 2, 2026 – a week-over-week increase of nearly 1,000. The prior week, the department announced the highest number of <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/respiratory-health/cold-and-flu" target="_blank" rel="noopener">flu cases</a> ever recorded in a single seven-day period.</p><p>In a statement, New York's Acting Commissioner of Health Dr. James McDonald noted the severity of this flu season compared to previous years.</p><p>"Almost 1,000 more people were admitted to a hospital during this most recent seven-day period compared to the prior week," he confirmed. "There is still time to get a <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/infectious-disease/vaccines" target="_blank" rel="noopener">flu shot</a>, and remember, flu can be treated with antiviral medication if started within 48 hours of symptom onset [as] your doctor deems appropriate."</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/health" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>CLICK HERE FOR MORE spread.</p><p>In a previous interview with Fox News Digital, Dr. Neil Maniar, professor of public health practice at Boston’s Northeastern University, emphasized that it's not too late to get the flu vaccine, as <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/infectious-disease" target="_blank" rel="noopener">peak season</a> typically occurs in January.</p><p>"The vaccine still provides protection against serious illness resulting from the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/health/flu-state-where-seasons-highly-contagious-variant-spreading-most" target="_blank" rel="noopener">subclade K variant</a> that seems to be going around," he said.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/newsletters?cmpid=fnfirstnl" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>CLICK HERE TO SIGN UP FOR OUR HEALTH fever, chills, headache, fatigue, cough, sore throat and runny nose.</p><p>A mutation of influenza A H3N2, called subclade K, has been detected as the culprit in <a "Certainly, in other parts of the world where this variant has been prevalent, it's caused some severe illness, and we're seeing an aggressive flu season already."</p><p>New York State recommends taking <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/healthy-living" target="_blank" rel="noopener">preventive actions</a> to avoid flu infection. These include washing hands often; avoiding touching the eyes, nose and mouth; avoiding close contact with sick people; cleaning and disinfecting objects and surfaces; and staying home when feeling sick.</p>

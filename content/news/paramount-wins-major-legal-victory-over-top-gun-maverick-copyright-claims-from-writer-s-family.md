<p>A federal appeals court on Friday tossed a copyright lawsuit claiming <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/person/tom-cruise" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Tom Cruise</a>’s 2022 hit sequel "Top Gun: Maverick" improperly used material from a magazine article that inspired the original 1986 film.</p><p>The 9th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals in California ruled that "Top Gun: Maverick" was not similar enough to Ehud Yonay's 1983 article "Top Guns," about the United States Navy Fighter Weapons School, for Paramount to have breached its 1983 agreement with the writer.</p><p>U.S. District Judge Percy Anderson for the Central District of California previously dismissed the case in 2024.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/us/us-regions/west/california" target="_blank" rel="noopener">California Magazine</a> published "Top Guns," an 11-page article written by Yonay in 1983, which described the Fighter Weapons School, later renamed the Strike Fighter Tactics Instructor Program, a Navy program that teaches advanced air-combat tactics.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/media/mediator-trumps-lawsuit-against-paramount-cbs-news-proposes-20-million-settlement" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>MEDIATOR IN TRUMP'S LAWSUIT AGAINST PARAMOUNT, CBS NEWS PROPOSES $20 MILLION SETTLEMENT </strong></a></p><p>Shortly after "Top Guns" was published, Yonay granted Paramount <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/entertainment/events/industry" target="_blank" rel="noopener">all rights to the article</a>, accepting a fixed payment with the condition that he would be credited in any movies "substantially based upon or adapted from" the article. </p><p>Paramount credited Yonay in the first movie, but following Yonay's death in 2012, his widow and son took over the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/entertainment/events/in-court" target="_blank" rel="noopener">copyright and terminated the agreement</a> with Paramount.</p><p>Two years later, in 2022, Paramount released "Top Gun: Maverick," a sequel to the original movie, which raked in $160.5 million in its first four days.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/media/how-trump-sued-cbs-won-60-minutes-settlement-rocked-media-world-explained" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>HOW TRUMP SUED CBS AND WON: THE '60 MINUTES' SETTLEMENT THAT ROCKED THE MEDIA WORLD, EXPLAINED</strong></a></p><p>Paramount did not compensate or credit the Yonays, spurring a lawsuit filed in the Central District of California.</p><p>The court found "Top Guns" and "Top Gun: Maverick" were not "substantially similar," as required to establish copyright infringement. </p><p>While the works had "some similarities," the court explained, those similarities were "based on unprotected elements" like facts about the Top Gun program, "general plot ideas, [and] familiar stock scenes and themes."</p><p>Paramount did not immediately respond to Fox News Digital's request for comment.</p><p>The Yonays could not immediately be reached for comment.</p>

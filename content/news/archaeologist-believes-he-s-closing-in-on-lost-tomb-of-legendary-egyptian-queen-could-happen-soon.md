<p>An <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/archaeology/ancient-egypt" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Egyptian archaeologist</a> believes he's narrowing in on the tomb of Queen Nefertiti, the legendary queen whose burial site has eluded historians for generations.</p><p>Zahi Hawass is the subject of a new documentary, "The Man with the Hat," which premiered on streaming platforms Tuesday. He served as Egypt's first Minister of Antiquities in 2011.</p><p>The film follows Hawass as he explores Egyptian history, including mysteries surrounding the deaths of Cleopatra and Tutankhamun — topics for which historians still lack clear answers.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/lifestyle/quizzes" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong><u>TEST YOURSELF WITH OUR LATEST LIFESTYLE QUIZ</u></strong></a></p><p>Hawass also discusses the prospect of finding the tomb of <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/archaeology" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Queen Nefertiti,</a> who lived from around 1370 B.C. to 1330 B.C.</p><p>"If I made this discovery, I think I would be happy to end my career with the most important discovery of the most important queen of Egypt," Hawass declares in <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/entertainment/genres/documentary" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the documentary</a>, according to Live Science.</p><p>Nefertiti was the principal wife of Pharaoh Akhenaten, the ruler who upended Egypt's religious customs away from polytheism and toward Atenism.</p><p>She played a central role in promoting the worship of Aten, the sun god.

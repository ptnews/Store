<p>Archaeologists recently identified the world's oldest-known poisoned arrowheads, a discovery that dates back around 60,000 years.</p><p>The poison was found on quartz arrowheads from Umhlatuzana Rock Shelter in KwaZulu-Natal, <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/travel" target="_blank" rel="noopener">South Africa,</a> SWNS reported. </p><p>The study's results, recently published in the journal Science Advances, involved Swedish and South African researchers.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/travel/rare-sea-reptile-makes-impressive-7000-mile-journey-tropics-northern-seas-back" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>RARE SEA REPTILE MAKES IMPRESSIVE 7,000-MILE JOURNEY FROM TROPICS TO NORTHERN SEAS AND BACK</strong></a></p><p>The Stone Age arrowheads were coated with poison from the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/planet-earth/plants" target="_blank" rel="noopener">South African plant gifbol</a>, a toxic bulb sometimes called the "poisonous onion."</p><p>The bulb contains buphanidrine and epibuphanisine, two chemical compounds that were found on the arrowheads.</p><p>The discovery represents the oldest known direct evidence of arrow poison, highlighting <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/great-outdoors/hunting" target="_blank" rel="noopener">advanced hunting skills</a> among Stone Age humans.</p><p>In a statement,

<p>Archaeologists in Italy recently unearthed a 2,000-year-old basilica <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/archaeology/ancient-rome" target="_blank" rel="noopener">linked to Vitruvius</a>, the legendary engineer long known as the father of architecture.</p><p>The building remains were found in Fano, a city roughly 150 miles northeast of Rome. The discovery was announced at a press conference with Italian officials on Jan. 19, according to Reuters.</p><p>Officials have identified the building as a basilica, or a public building. Basilicas were largely used for civic, not religious, purposes before Rome <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/christianity" target="_blank" rel="noopener">adopted Christianity</a>.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/travel/self-healing-concrete-buried-pompeii-site-reveals-secret-romes-enduring-structures" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>SELF-HEALING CONCRETE? BURIED POMPEII SITE REVEALS SECRET BEHIND ROME'S ENDURING STRUCTURES</strong></a></p><p>Photos from the site show archaeologists working around the remains of the ancient stone building. The basilica had a rectangular layout, said officials, with 10 columns on its longer sides and four on its shorter ones.</p><p>The basilica is the only building that experts can connect to Vitruvius with complete certainty.</p><p>Vitruvius was born around 80–70 B.C. and died in 15 B.C. He is the renowned author of "De architectura," an ancient text about architecture.</p><p>The text consists of 10 treatises on architecture, engineering and urban planning, and is the oldest <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/lifestyle/lifestyle-books" target="_blank" rel="noopener">surviving work written</a> on the subject.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/lifestyle/quizzes" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong><u>TEST YOURSELF WITH OUR LATEST LIFESTYLE QUIZ</u></strong></a></p><p>Leonardo da Vinci's famous "<a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/archaeology" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Vitruvian Man</a>" drawing is a nod to Vitruvius, who also influenced such architects as Christopher Wren and Andrea Palladio.</p><p>Regional archaeological superintendent Andrea Pessina told reporters that officials "have [an] absolute match" between the discovery and the basilica described in Vitruvius's writings.</p><p>"There are few certainties in archaeology ... but we were impressed by the precision [of the match]," Pessina added.</p><p>Archaeologists plan to continue working at the site to find more remnants. They said they hope to <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/travel/general/museums-exhibits" target="_blank" rel="noopener">show the site to the public</a> one day.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/newsletters?cmpid=fnfirstnl" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong><u>CLICK HERE TO SIGN UP FOR OUR LIFESTYLE NEWSLETTER</u></strong></a></p><p>Italian Culture Minister Alessandro Giuli called the discovery "a sensational finding."</p><p>"[This is] something that our grandchildren will be talking about," said Giuli.</p><p>Luca Serfilippi, the mayor of Fano, called the find "the discovery of the century," and mentioned that researchers have searched for the basilica for centuries.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/lifestyle" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong><u>CLICK HERE FOR MORE LIFESTYLE STORIES</u></strong></a></p><p>"Scientists and researchers have been searching for this basilica for over 500 years," Serfilippi said. </p><p>The unique find follows a long list of discoveries related to ancient Rome in recent months.</p><p>Last fall, archaeologists found a massive stone basin in Italy belonging to the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/travel/archaeologists-uncover-massive-stone-ruins-ancient-city-once-rivaled-rome" target="_blank" rel="noopener">city of Gabii, </a>a once-powerful rival of Rome.</p><p>Weeks earlier, the Sicilian regional government announced the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/travel/divers-uncover-extraordinary-military-treasure-tied-legendary-roman-battle" target="_blank" rel="noopener">discovery of a helmet</a> from the Battle of the Aegates in 241 B.C.</p><p><i>Reuters contributed reporting.</i></p>

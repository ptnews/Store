<p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/topic/primates" target="_blank" rel="noopener">Several monkeys</a> continue to be on the loose in St. Louis, Missouri, according to city officials — and now, the city's Department of Health has called off its search for the roaming animals.</p><p>The animals were first reported near a park on the city’s north side on Thursday, Department of Health spokesperson Willie Springer told The Associated Press.</p><p>The monkeys are believed to be vervet monkeys.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/lifestyle/wild-bear-makes-very-polite-surprise-visit-california-zoo-before-returning-forest" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>WILD BEAR MAKES ‘VERY POLITE’ SURPRISE VISIT TO CALIFORNIA ZOO BEFORE RETURNING TO FOREST</strong></a></p><p>Vervet monkeys are medium-sized primates native to Africa.</p><p>They're known for their grayish-green fur and dark facial features, according to the African Wildlife Foundation.</p><p>Vervet monkeys that live near human populations are sometimes considered pests — as they <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/food-drink" target="_blank" rel="noopener">may steal food</a>, raid crops, and take other items, often leading to lethal control measures, the foundation noted.</p><p>At this time, it is unclear exactly how many monkeys are on the loose — and who owns them.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/newsletters?cmpid=fnfirstnl"><strong><u>CLICK HERE TO SIGN UP FOR OUR LIFESTYLE NEWSLETTER</u></strong></a></p><p>Springer said the largest number of monkeys reported together so far is four, suggesting there is not a large group roaming the city. </p><p>Ownership claims are also unlikely, as residents are not legally permitted to own monkeys in St. Louis.</p><p>Officials, including primate experts at the <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/travel/vacation-destinations/st-louis" target="_blank" rel="noopener">St. Louis Zoo,</a> had been working to locate the animals.</p><p>As residents are keeping an eye out for the monkeys, some <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/tech/understanding-ai" target="_blank" rel="noopener">AI-generated images</a> and false reports have complicated efforts to track and capture the monkeys, officials said.</p><p>"It’s been a lot in regard to AI and what’s genuine and what’s not," Springer said. "People are just having fun … I don’t think anyone means harm."</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/lifestyle"><strong><u>CLICK HERE FOR MORE LIFESTYLE STORIES</u></strong></a></p><p>The city’s health department said in a Facebook post that residents with firsthand information or sightings should report them directly to the City of St. Louis Citizens’ Service Bureau. </p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/lifestyle/quizzes" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong><u>TEST YOURSELF WITH OUR LATEST LIFESTYLE QUIZ</u></strong></a></p><p>City officials have urged residents not to approach <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/science/wild-nature" target="_blank" rel="noopener">the monkeys</a> and to contact authorities instead.</p><p>Fox News Digital reached out to St. Louis Animal Control and the St. Louis Zoo for further comment.</p><p>On Monday, the city shifted its efforts from investigation to enforcement of its wild animal ordinance, according to Justin Hauser, the health department’s environmental health bureau chief, as <a href="https://fox2now.com/news/missouri/st-louis-city-has-ceased-its-search-for-vervet-monkeys/" target="_blank" rel="nofollow noopener">Fox 2</a> reported. </p><p>The city is aware of sightings and videos shared on social media, Hauser also said.

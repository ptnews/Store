<p>Cabbage is emerging as one of the defining <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/food-drink/food/food-trends" target="_blank" rel="noopener">food trends</a> of 2026.</p><p>Once known for boiled dinners and bland side dishes, the vegetable is being reimagined as a versatile, affordable kitchen staple.</p><p>Erin Clarke, a Wisconsin-based <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/food-drink/food/cookbooks" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cookbook author</a> and founder of WellPlated, told Fox News Digital the shift reflects changing priorities among home cooks, including cost, nutrition and flexibility in the kitchen.</p><p><a href="https://https://www.foxnews.com/health/how-much-red-meat-too-much-experts-weigh-food-pyramid-updates" target="_blank" rel="noopener"><strong>HOW MUCH RED MEAT IS TOO MUCH? EXPERTS WEIGH IN ON FOOD PYRAMID UPDATES</strong></a></p><p>"Cabbage is experiencing a renaissance because home cooks have finally discovered what chefs have known for years," she said. "When cooked properly, it's absolutely delicious."</p><p>Clarke said the vegetable’s resurgence also reflects a broader move toward practical, everyday ingredients that still deliver flavor.</p><p>"There's also a growing appreciation for vegetables that are budget-friendly, available year-round and incredibly versatile," she said. </p><p>"Cabbage checks all those boxes while also fitting into various dietary preferences."</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/newsletters?cmpid=fnfirstnl"><strong><u>CLICK HERE TO SIGN UP FOR OUR LIFESTYLE NEWSLETTER</u></strong></a></p><p>Clarke said texture and <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/food-drink/food/cooking-basics" target="_blank" rel="noopener">cooking method</a> make all the difference for people who think they dislike cabbage.</p><p>She recommended slicing it thin and cooking it over high heat until it develops golden-brown, caramelized edges, which bring out its natural sweetness.</p><p>New Jersey–based dietitian Erin Palinski-Wade, author of "The 2-Day Diabetes Diet," said cabbage offers notable <a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/nutrition-and-fitness/nutrition" target="_blank" rel="noopener">health benefits</a> compared to many commonly eaten vegetables.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/lifestyle"><strong><u>CLICK HERE FOR MORE LIFESTYLE STORIES</u></strong></a></p><p>"Cabbage is a cruciferous, low-calorie vegetable that, when compared to a popular alternative such as lettuce, provides higher levels of vitamin C, folate, and potassium," Palinski-Wade said.</p><p>Research has linked compounds found in cruciferous vegetables to anti-inflammatory and antioxidant effects, along with potential benefits for gut health and protection against certain cancers and <a href="https://https://www.foxnews.com/category/health/heart-health" target="_blank" rel="noopener">heart-related conditions</a>, she added.</p><p><a href="https://www.foxnews.com/category/lifestyle/quizzes"><strong><u>TEST YOURSELF WITH OUR LATEST LIFESTYLE QUIZ</u></strong></a></p><p>While cabbage offers a range of benefits, Palinski-Wade said it may not be ideal for everyone, particularly those with digestive sensitivities.</p><p>She noted that cabbage can cause gas or bloating for some people due to its high fiber content, especially when eaten raw or in large amounts.</p><p>Palinski-Wade said cooking cabbage can make it easier to digest while still preserving many of its nutritional benefits.</p><p>She suggested lightly sautéing or steaming cabbage, rather than boiling it, to help retain the nutrients while improving texture and tolerance.</p>
